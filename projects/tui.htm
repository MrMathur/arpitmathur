_ABOUT THE PROJECT:

As part of the course titled “Tangible User Interfaces”, my group and I had to develop a prototpye tackling the problems faced by children afflicted with Cerebral Palsy.

We designed ProximiTREE, a tangible device that teaches the CP patients the skill of subitization. As part of the project, we had to gain insights through a user study, convert them into a design solution, and develop a prototype.

I conducted this project along with my colleagues Ashutosh Agrawal, Abhishek Kanthed and Nilotpal Nayan.

RESEARCH

Shisho Sarothi is a training center in Guwahati, India, with a vision of an inclusive world, where children with special needs are adequately trained and empowered in order to have an equal footing in the highly inaccessible setup of the world.

Our batch paid a visit to understand how we could make a difference to the state of affairs as desiginers of future technologies.

We spoke to Mr. Armaan Ali (the director) and various teachers for specialized training of the children to know more about the problems faced by the children.

To respect the privacy of the students, we did not click any photographs during the study.

1. USER RESEARCH FINDINGS

With particular focus on Cerebral Palsy, 9 functional units for education, physical rehabilitation, skill-development and leisure activities were observed. Note-taking, videography and photography methods were used to record the data. Some major insights obtained from primary research are-

Focus on skill-development based on five verticals- Cognitive, Physical, Self-health, Social and Communication skills.

Development of cognitive skills using tactile media through activities like joining the dots, shape and color sorting, flashcards, painting etc.

Use of computer software limited to children with satisfactory motor skills.

Use of Clevy keyboard and trackball for mouse to interact with the computer.

Computer training involves typing, data-entry, powerpoint presentations and other educational applications in the form of games.

2. LITERATURE RESEARCH

What is subitizing?

Subitizing is the ability to identify the quantity of a certain amount of items that are presented in too short a time to make counting possible.

Why do CP patients have problems with subitization?

Children with CP do not show a qualitatively different developmental trajectory but that this trajectory is merely delayed.

What causes this problem?

The ‘visuospatial deficit’ hypothesis, holds that children with CP are hindered in their perception and memorization of spatial patterns.

How can we solve it?

The Gestalt law of proximity states that "objects or shapes that are close to one another appear to form groups". Tangible technologies have generated a wealth of novel opportunities to support children’s learning. Digital effects might be used to encourage certain actions and states over others, thereby allowing a balance to made between efficiency and innovation. Thus, tangible interfaces can be effective in supporting children’s learning.

PROBLEM STATEMENT AND OBJECTIVES

"To design an interactive tangible user interface that teaches the gestalt perception of proximity to children suffering with Cerebral Palsy. It should be effective and relatable to the real world and should, to its best ability, help the children to "understand" subitizing. The vision is to develop numeracy in CP children to increase their arithmetical abilities and improve their social skills in the process."

3. OBJECTIVES

To develop the gestalt perception of proximity among CP children.

Teach them using instances from the real world so they are directly connected to the real world and it does not remain an abstract concept for them.

To develop within them the skill of subitizing.

To inculcate a sense of helping other people (volunteering) thereby improving social skills..

To keep them entertained and happy.

IDEATION AND CONCEPTUALIZATION

Considerably less research has been done towards developing gestalt perception of proximity in children as its an innate psychological ability but is impaired in CP children. Initial ideation sessions were directed towards identifying real-life scenarios involving application of the proximity principle. Another strategy used was to ideate upon the various ways in which this ability could be tested in another person. Instructions for learning can be then be derived from the test. After narrowing down to a specific scenario, principles and characteristics of TUI were applied to develop the concept.

4. CONCEPT

Efforts were put in to combine real-life metaphors to the learning process while keeping it entertaining and engaging. Games have shown to assist learning in children. They are fun, engaging and make learning enjoyable for children. Various real-life scenarios were explored that can be implemented in the game. Finally, the scenario of “helping a farmer pluck fruits from a tree” was chosen.

The final concept involves an interactive game, with a screen-less interface that shall also try to improve the motor skills of the children. The interface consists of a vertical interactive surface in the form of a tree with fruits hanging on it in different clusters. The fruits hang in a cluster at different orientation and angles in a non-canonical arrangement such that the relative distance between them is less. Each cluster is enclosed by LED lights. There’s a basket below the tree to collect the fruits. The final goal of the game is to help the farmer to collect all fruits from a particular cluster in the basket thereby learning the concept of grouping together objects close to each other. By volunteering to help out the farmer, the child also learns a sense of responsibility towards other human beings thereby improving their social skills in the process.

The child initially picks a cluster of fruits of his choice by plucking a fruit from that cluster. The light around that cluster flashes for a short interval to indicate the chosen cluster. The child is then required to pluck all the fruits from that particular cluster and place them on the basket. If he tries to plucks a fruit from a different cluster, the mechanism doesn’t enable him to do so and the light from the chosen cluster flashes in regular intervals. If there is an inactivity of 30 seconds from the child, the light from the chosen cluster again flashes to highlight that cluster. Upon collecting all the fruits, the farmer is happy and thanks for the child for his help.

The electronic circuit of the game is as shown:

The arduino is attached to the tree, and connected to a laptop via a nrf module for data transfer. The arduino senses the presense or absence of a ball from its position through light sensors. The values were caliberated through trial and error by placing and replacing the balls multiple times from the tree.

The LEDs were attached by surrounding each of the clusters of the balls to demonstrate the solution (ideally, the balls should light up on their own). According to the logic of the solution (as in the flow above), the LEDs would light up using the response from the Arduino.

When all the clusters have been removed from the tree, the arduino device sends a signal to the laptop to start the video of a farmer waving and thanking the child for collecting his fruit.
